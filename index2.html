Introduction to Marketing

What is Marketing?

Marketing is a process by which companies create value for customers and build strong customer relationships to capture value from customers in return.

Marketing Process

The marketing process involves understanding customer needs, designing customer-driven strategies, constructing marketing programs, building relationships, and capturing value.

Understanding Customer Needs

Needs: States of deprivation including:

  • Physical needs: food, clothing, warmth, safety
  • Social needs: belonging and affection
  • Individual needs: knowledge and self-expression

Wants: The form that needs take as they are shaped by culture and individual personality.

Demands: Wants backed by buying power.

Marketing Myopia

Marketing myopia is the mistake of focusing only on existing wants and losing sight of underlying consumer needs.

Marketing Mix & Process

Marketing Offerings

Marketing offerings can include:

  • Product: Physical goods or services
  • Service: Intangible activities or benefits
  • Information: Knowledge and data
  • Experience: Memorable events
  • Person: Individual representation

Designing a Customer-Driven Marketing Strategy

Marketing management is the art and science of choosing target markets and building profitable relationships with them.

Selecting Customers to Serve:

  • Market segmentation: Dividing markets into segments of customers
  • Target marketing: Deciding which segments to serve

Choosing a Value Proposition:

A brand's value proposition is the set of benefits or values a company promises to deliver to customers to satisfy their needs.

Marketing Management Orientations

Marketing Management Concepts

There are five alternative concepts under which organizations conduct their marketing activities:

Production Concept: Consumers will favor products that are available and highly affordable.

Product Concept: Consumers favor products that offer the most quality, performance, and features. Focus is on continuous product improvements.

Selling Concept: Consumers will not buy enough of the firm's products unless it undertakes a large-scale selling and promotion effort.

Marketing Concept: Knowing the needs and wants of the target markets and delivering the desired satisfactions better than competitors do.

Societal Marketing Concept: Making good marketing decisions by considering consumers' wants and long-term interests, company's requirements, and society's long-run interests.

Strategic Planning & BCG Matrix

Strategic Planning

Strategic planning is the process of developing and maintaining a strategic fit between the organization's goals and capabilities and its changing marketing opportunities.

Steps in Strategic Planning:

  • Defining a market-oriented mission
  • Setting company objectives and goals
  • Designing the business portfolio
  • Planning marketing and other functional strategies

BCG Growth-Share Matrix

The Boston Consulting Group (BCG) growth-share matrix is a planning tool that uses graphical representations of a company's products and services to help decide what to keep, sell, or invest in.

STARS: Products in high-growth markets with sizable market share. Generate high income but consume large cash. Companies should invest significantly.

CASH COWS: Products in low-growth areas with large market share. Typically leading products in mature markets. Companies should milk these for cash to reinvest.

QUESTION MARKS: Products in high-growth markets but with small market share. Grow fast but consume large resources. Companies should analyze frequently.

DOGS: Products with low market share and low growth. Don't generate much cash. Prime candidates for divestiture.

Growth Strategies

Product/Market Expansion Grid

A tool for identifying company growth opportunities through four strategies:

Market Penetration: Growth by increasing sales to current market segments without changing the product. Example: Add new stores in current market areas.

Market Development: Growth by identifying and developing new market segments for current products. Example: Review new demographic markets.

Product Development: Growth strategy that offers new or modified products to existing market segments. Example: Moving into new product categories.

Diversification: Growth strategy through starting up or acquiring businesses outside the company's current products and markets.

Marketing Environment Analysis

The Marketing Environment

The marketing environment includes actors and forces outside marketing that affect marketing management's ability to build successful customer relationships.

Microenvironment: Actors close to the company that affect its ability to serve customers:

  • The company itself
  • Suppliers
  • Marketing intermediaries
  • Customer markets
  • Competitors
  • Publics

Macroenvironment

Larger societal forces that affect the microenvironment:

Political Factors: Government policy, political stability, foreign trade, corruption, tax policy.

Economic Factors: Economic growth, disposable income, unemployment rate, exchange rate, inflation rate, spending patterns.

Social Factors: Population growth rate, age distribution, income level, careers, lifestyle, cultural barriers.

Technological Factors: Innovation, technological change, technological awareness.

Natural Environment: Shortages of raw materials, increased pollution, government intervention, environmentally sustainable strategies.

Legal Factors: Discrimination laws, employment laws, consumer protection laws, copyright laws.

Consumer Behavior Factors

Factors Influencing Consumer Behavior

Cultural Factors:

  • Culture: Learned values, perceptions, wants, and behavior from family and institutions
  • Subculture: Groups with shared value systems based on common life experiences
  • Social Classes: Society's relatively permanent divisions with similar values and behaviors

Social Factors:

  • Membership Groups: Groups with direct influence
  • Aspirational Groups: Groups an individual wishes to belong to
  • Reference Groups: Groups that form comparison for attitudes/behavior
  • Family: Most important consumer-buying organization
  • Social Roles and Status

Personal Factors

  • Age and life-cycle stage
  • Occupation
  • Economic situation
  • Lifestyle: Pattern of living expressed in psychographics (AIOs - activities, interests, opinions)
  • Personality: Unique psychological characteristics

Psychological Factors

  • Motivation: Needs that direct behavior
  • Perception: Process of selecting, organizing, and interpreting information
  • Learning: Changes in behavior from experience
  • Beliefs and Attitudes: Descriptive thoughts and evaluations

Consumer Decision Process

Buyer Decision Making Process

Need Recognition: Occurs when buyer recognizes a problem or need triggered by internal or external stimuli.

Information Search: Sources include:

  • Personal sources: family and friends
  • Commercial sources: advertising, Internet
  • Public sources: mass media, consumer organizations
  • Experiential sources: handling, examining, using the product

Evaluation of Alternatives: How consumer processes information to arrive at brand choices.

Purchase Decision: The act of buying the most preferred brand, affected by attitudes of others and unexpected situational factors.

Post-Purchase Decision: Satisfaction or dissatisfaction based on the relationship between expectations and perceived performance.

Adoption Process for New Products

The mental process from first learning about an innovation to final regular use:

  • Awareness
  • Interest
  • Evaluation
  • Trial
  • Adoption

Adopter Categories:

  • Innovators
  • Early Adopters
  • Early Majority
  • Late Majority
  • Laggards

Market Segmentation

Segmenting Consumer Markets

Geographic Segmentation: Dividing market into different geographical units (nations, regions, states, counties, cities).

Demographic Segmentation: Dividing market based on age, gender, family size, life cycle, income, occupation, education, religion, race, generation, nationality.

Psychographic Segmentation: Dividing buyers based on social class, lifestyle, or personality traits.

Behavioral Segmentation: Dividing buyers based on knowledge, attitudes, uses, or responses:

  • Occasions: When they get idea to buy, make purchase, or use item
  • Benefits: Different benefits sought from product
  • User Status: Nonusers, ex-users, potential users, first-time users, regular users
  • Loyalty Status: Completely loyal, somewhat loyal, no loyalty

Requirements for Effective Segmentation

Market segments must be:

  • Measurable: Size, purchasing power, and characteristics can be measured
  • Accessible: Effectively reached and served
  • Substantial: Large and profitable enough to serve
  • Differentiable: Conceptually distinguishable and respond differently
  • Actionable: Effective programs can be designed for attracting and serving

Differentiation & Positioning

Product Positioning

Product position is the way the product is defined by consumers on important attributes—the place the product occupies in consumers' minds relative to competing products based on perceptions, impressions, and feelings.

Positioning Maps: Show consumer perceptions of brands versus competing products on important buying dimensions.

Choosing a Positioning Strategy

Identifying Competitive Advantages: Building a position by providing superior value through:

  • Product differentiation
  • Service differentiation
  • Channel differentiation
  • People differentiation
  • Image differentiation

Choosing the Right Competitive Advantage: Differences should be important, distinctive, superior, communicable, preemptive, affordable, and profitable.

Positioning Strategies

More-for-More: Providing the most upscale product/service with higher price for higher costs and prestige.

More for the Same: Attacking competitors by offering comparable quality at lower price.

The Same for Less: Offering the same for less—a powerful value proposition.

Less for Much Less: Offering less and costing less for markets that don't need "the very best."

More for Less: The winning but difficult-to-sustain proposition of offering more for less.
